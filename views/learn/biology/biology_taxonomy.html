Taxonomy


Taxonomy is a hierarchical system for classifying and identifying organisms. This system was developed by Swedish scientist Carolus Linnaeus in the 8th century.

Binomial Nomenclature

Linnaeus's taxonomy system has two main features that contribute to its ease of use in naming and grouping organism. The first is the use of binomial nomenclature. This mans that an organism's scientific name is comprised of a combination of two terms.

This terms are the genus name and the species or epithet. Both of these terms are italicized and the genus name is also capitalized. For example, the scientific name for humans is Homo sapiens. The genus name is Homo and the species is sapiens. These terms are unique and no other species can have this same name.

Classification Categories

The second feature of Linnaeus's taxonomy system that simplifies organism classification is the ordering of species into broad categories. The major categories include: Kingdom, Phylum, Class, Order, Family, Genus, and Species. These categories have been updated to include Domain in the taxonomic hierarchy. Domain is the broadest category and organisms are qrouped primarily according to differences in ribosomal RNA structure.

A good aid for remembering taxonomic categories is the mnemonic device: Do Keep Plates Clean Or Family Gets Sick.

Taxonomic categories can be further divided into intermediate categories such as subphya, suborders, superfamilies, and superclasses.

An example of this taxonomy scheme is below. It includes the eight main categories along with subcategories and supercategories. The superkingdom rank is the same as the Domain rank.
